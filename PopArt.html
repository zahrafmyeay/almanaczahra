Pop Art

Pop Art

Concept

Not focusing on highbrow subjects or abstract emotions, pop artists embraced imagery from popular culture. Pop Art uses content like comic strips, advertisements, and everyday objects like soup cans all elevated to the status of museum-worthy pieces. Pop art often incorporated reproduction techniques like screenprinting, mirroring the mass production of the very items it depicted. This movement, led by Andy Warhol and Roy Lichtenstein, aimed to bridge the gap between "high" and "low" culture, raise conversations about consumerism, mass media, and the nature of art itself. This movement makes art more accessible and also brings more notice into media in society which is often depicted ironically.

Andy Warhol

Artist

As an artist, filmmaker, editor, writer and record producer, Warhol has worked in so many different media formats that beginning to analyze his output can be overwhelming. (Elizabeth 2014) He was born in Pittsburgh in Pennsylvania in the USA as a third son of Andrej Warhola and Julia Justina Zavacka. His original name was Andrew Warhola, but he created his artistic name when he came to New York in 1949. The date of his birth is not exactly known. In many sources, we can find dates between 1928 - 1931, but the most stated date is 6 July 1928. (Tereze 2013)

Roy Lichtenstein

Artist

In the early 1960s Roy Lichtenstein was one of a group of American artists whose work came to be categorized within the larger movement known as Pop Art. This dissertation reconsiders the work Lichtenstein produced in the period between 1961 and 1965, and challenges those accounts which have maintained that the artist's relation to his comic book and advertising imagery was as simple and controlled as his painting style. In contrast, through a careful reading of Lichtenstein's early Pop paintings, and an examination of their historical context, the dissertation identifies in the artist's seemingly uninflected imagery a set of wholly ambivalent responses to issues of vision, repetition, and technology. (Eric and Lobel 1999)
